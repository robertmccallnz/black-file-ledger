In a green Marlborough valley, two white domes sit on the plain like something dropped from a cold sky. They are not decoration. They are infrastructure. And what they hear, they share — not with New Zealand, but with an alliance built for empire. This is not a conspiracy. It is a documented arrangement. The game is already running. You are already a piece.

Plot Summary — The Long Game

The premise: New Zealand is not a neutral country. It is a franchise. A junior partner in a signals-intelligence empire that runs from Fort Meade to GCHQ to Pine Gap to Waihopai. The arrangement was not voted on. It was inherited, then maintained by successive governments of every flavour — because the people who benefit from it are not the kind of people who appear on a ballot.

Six characters move through this story. They are not heroes and villains. They are institutional types — the kind of people who fill every surveillance state, every boardroom, every union hall, every press gallery. They have met before. They will meet again. Across Issues 001–006, their paths cross at every crisis point in NZ's intelligence history — and each time, the workers lose a little more, and the domes keep listening.

The game: Treat this like a board. The Capitalists move first. They always do. The question is whether anyone else gets a turn.

A note on the game mechanics: In Monopoly, the banker always wins — because they made the rules, print the money, and own the board. New Zealand's intelligence architecture operates on similar principles. The only difference is that Monopoly tells you it's a game.
The Black File Game — Mechanics for Carnivore Capitalists

Welcome to the Board, Mr Sovereign Fund

This is the game as it is actually played — not as civics textbooks describe it. Six player types. One valley. Five Eyes watching everything. Here are your moves:

  • The State (plays every turn): Share intelligence with allies. Receive plausible deniability in return. Upgrade the domes. Decline all OIA requests about the domes. Repeat.
  • The Capitalist (bonus turn on election years): Fund both major parties. Rotate preferred faces. Ensure no structural change occurs regardless of result. Eat well.
  • The Organiser (1 action per decade, if lucky): Build a coalition. Have it surveilled. Watch it fracture. Rebuild. Publish a pamphlet. Have it archived.
  • The Worker (no bonus turns): Pay taxes that fund the domes. Receive no intelligence. Wonder why the cost of living keeps climbing. Not on the board, technically.
  • The Media (wildcard): Report the procedural issue. Miss the structural story. Publish the press release. Win an award for the procedural issue.
  • Victory condition: There is no victory condition for workers. This is a feature, not a bug. The game was designed by the winner.

The Cast — File 00

These six characters recur across all issues. They are composites — drawn from institutional types, not named individuals. Each carries a worldview shaped by who employs them, who they owe favours to, and what they were told to forget.

The cast — pencil and charcoal, all issues

Character 01

Retired public servant. Forty years in the filing rooms of Wellington bureaucracy. Knows where everything is. Knows what is missing. Knows the difference — which is more dangerous than either. Lives alone in a flat above a second-hand bookshop. Drinks too much instant coffee. Answers no phone calls from numbers he doesn't recognise, which is most of them now.

His role across the issues: memory. When the official record has been sanitised, The Archivist holds the original. He didn't plan to be a dissident. He just never threw anything away.

Character 02
The Organiser

Third-generation union delegate. Her grandmother was on the waterfront in 1951. Her mother was on the picket line in 1991. She learned early that the system does not reform itself — it is pushed. Surveilled since her twenties, which she discovered only by filing an OIA request that came back with her own name in it, half-redacted.

Her role across the issues: class consciousness. She is the only character who correctly names what the domes are for. Nobody in power quotes her.

Labour movement Surveilled Correct about everything
Character 03
The Witness

Former low-level analyst. Not intelligence — contracting. Was in the room when a report came through. Understood what it described. Filed a concern through proper channels. Was transferred. Filed again. Was let go on a restructure. Has not worked in the sector since. Moves carefully. Speaks to very few people.

His role across the issues: the cost of knowing. He is what happens when an individual conscience collides with an institutional one. The institution wins on points. He carries the bruising.

Whistleblower-adjacent Isolated Uncited
Character 04
The Security Professional

Senior figure in the intelligence community. Educated at a good school, then a better university, then a placement that wasn't advertised publicly. Has never doubted the framework — not because he lacks intelligence, but because the framework has always rewarded him. Serves successive governments of different flavours with identical policy advice.

His role across the issues: institutional continuity. He is how the arrangement survives elections. Calm, competent, and structurally immune to accountability.

Character 05
The Editor

Independent journalist. Used to work for a masthead. Still grieves for it. Now publishes on Substack and grants income and sheer bloody-mindedness. Has broken three significant stories in ten years. Two were reported as procedural issues by the press gallery. One was ignored entirely. Keeps a wall of clippings that charts the distance between what happened and what was reported.

Her role across the issues: language as politics. She is the one who notices when a word changes — when "detention" becomes "transfer" becomes "process." She names the substitution. Not enough people read her.

The Diplomat

Long-serving official. Has represented New Zealand at enough Five Eyes liaison meetings to know exactly how the arrangement works. Retires to a board position at a consultancy that advises on "geopolitical risk" — which is what the private sector calls intelligence, when they're paying for it rather than providing it.

His role across the issues: the revolving door. He is the hinge between public service and private capture. Every empire has one. Ours wears merino.

File 01 — The Domes

The Waihopai station sits outside Blenheim in the Waihopai Valley. Operated by the Government Communications Security Bureau — the GCSB — it intercepts satellite communications as part of the ECHELON network: the signals-intelligence arm of the Five Eyes alliance.

The GCSB's own published history confirms New Zealand's participation in the UKUSA agreement. That agreement was not put to a public vote. It was inherited, then expanded, then quietly normalised across governments of every ideological flavour — because the people who benefit from normalisation are not on the ballot.

Source: Government Communications Security Bureau, Our History — see endnotes.

"Every institution that survives long enough starts to look like furniture. Waihopai has been there since 1989. A whole generation has grown up treating it as background. That's the point. Infrastructure looks inevitable until someone asks who built it, and why. I've been asking that question for thirty years. The filing cabinet where the answer lives has a lock I was never given a key for. I know because I filed the key's paperwork."

File 02 — Operation Chaos

In 1976, a publication called The C.I.A. in New Zealand: Operation Chaos circulated through the NZ left. It described CIA files on political activity in New Zealand — surveillance of socialist and anti-war movements tracked as threats to US interests.

Source: National Library of New Zealand — see endnotes.

The Organiser — File 02 Statement
"My grandmother's name was in that pamphlet. Not her real name — a description. 'Labour activist, Auckland waterfront connections.' She was a cleaner. She went to meetings. She signed her name to things. That was enough to make the map. They weren't afraid of her specifically. They were afraid of what she represented — which is workers deciding, together, that the arrangement is unacceptable. That fear is constant. The technology just gets more expensive."

File 03 — Torture by Proxy

In 2019, the Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security confirmed that New Zealand agencies had lines of connection to the CIA's post-9/11 detention, interrogation, and rendition programme. Intelligence derived from people confirmed to have been tortured had been received and used by NZ agencies. No one was prosecuted. The official framing was procedural.

The Witness was not named in the report. He was not interviewed for the report. He had stopped speaking about what he knew by then — not because he had been silenced formally, but because he had learned that the channels for speaking lead nowhere that helps.

Source: Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security, 2019 — see endnotes.

The Witness — File 03 Statement
"There is a person at the end of every protocol. A body in every intelligence product. I reported through the correct process. The correct process did not protect the person. It protected the process. I should have understood earlier that this was its function."
The Security Professional — File 03 Counterstatement
"The review process identified procedural shortfalls and recommended corrective action. New Zealand's intelligence obligations are real and significant. Small-state security posture requires meaningful alliance integration. The oversight framework exists precisely to catch and correct these issues. It caught them. It corrected them. That is the system working."
Translation: "The torture happened. The paperwork was wrong. We fixed the paperwork. The alliance continues. Please don't ask about the person."

The Editor Speaks — Language as the First Battleground

The Editor
"The story ran as: 'NZ agencies had information-sharing concerns.' Not: 'New Zealand's intelligence service helped a torture system function.' Language is not neutral. The gap between those two sentences is a political choice — made by editors, by subeditors, by institutional habit, by the knowledge that the Security Professional's office is three floors above the press gallery and everyone has to coexist at the same reception drinks. I no longer attend reception drinks. This is why I have to fund my own work."

The Diplomat Closes the File — For Now

The Diplomat
"New Zealand maintains a principled foreign policy. We support human rights. We also maintain our alliance obligations. These positions are held simultaneously — with composure, and without examination. I have held them simultaneously for thirty-two years. I am very well. I have a very good wine cellar. The merino is excellent this year."
